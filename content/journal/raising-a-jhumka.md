A jhumka begins as nothing more than a flat brass disc. You would never guess, holding that small bright coin in your palm, that it wants to become a bell.

## Finding the dome

The disc is laid over a wooden doming block — a worn old thing, dished with years of use — and tapped with a rounded hammer, a little at a time. The trick is patience. Strike too hard in one place and the metal puckers; you have to move around the dome, turning it between blows, coaxing the curve up evenly.

I know it is ready by the sound. A dome that has settled evenly **rings** when you tap it — a clear, small note. An uneven one answers with a dull thud. So I work until the brass sings back.

> The metal tells you when it is finished. You only have to listen for it.

## Building the marigold

Once the two bells match, the flower begins. Lengths of fine wire are twisted into petals and soldered around the rim, one at a time, so that no two marigolds sit exactly alike. This is the slow part — and the part I love most. The marigold has hung over every doorway and floated in every festival bowl I can remember, and to build one petal by petal feels like keeping a small promise.

Last of all, the freshwater pearls are knotted on, hung at a length that lets them swing without tangling.

## The nine hours

People are sometimes surprised that a pair of earrings can take nine hours. But the hours are not waiting — they are doming, listening, twisting, soldering, knotting. They are the reason the pair you receive feels like it was made for a person, not a shelf.

I make these in pairs over two evenings. The second bell always sits a little quieter than the first — and I have learned to let it.
