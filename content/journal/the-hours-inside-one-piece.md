When someone picks up a piece and turns over the price, I always wish I could show them the hours folded up inside it. Not to justify anything — just because the hours *are* the piece. They are the part you cannot see, and the part that makes it worth keeping.

## Where the time goes

Take the [Peacock Haar](/shop/peacock-haar). On paper it is a necklace of ceramic beads. In hours it is:

- rolling and piercing each bead by hand
- glazing, firing, and often re-firing to coax the colour out
- laying the beads out and grading them from teal to gold
- stringing the whole thing — and restringing it, more than once, until it falls in a clean curve

That is around twenty-one hours, spread over many evenings. The [Marigold Jhumka](/shop/marigold-jhumka) is nine. A small pair of studs might be three or four. None of those hours are waiting around; they are all hands-on-the-bench.

> The hours are the part you cannot see, and the part that makes it worth keeping.

## Why I write them down

On every piece, I tell you how long it took. I do it because handmade has become a word people use loosely, and I want mine to mean something exact. *This many hours, these specific materials, this pair of hands.*

Slow is not a marketing line here. It is just how long it takes to do it properly — and, quietly, it is the reason each piece can only ever be [one of a kind](/journal/one-of-a-kind). There are only so many evenings.
