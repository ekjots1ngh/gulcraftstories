The Peacock Haar took longer to make than anything else I have ever attempted — and longer still to get *right*. This is the story of where it came from, and the hours it asked for.

## An hour before the rain

In my grandmother's courtyard, peacocks would arrive in the heavy, held-breath hour before the monsoon broke. The light then is strange and green-gold, and a peacock's neck in that light is a colour I have spent years trying to name. Not teal, not blue, not green — all three, shifting as the bird turns.

That colour is the whole reason this piece exists.

## Three weeks at the kiln

Enamel is powdered glass. You paint it onto brass, then fire it in a small kiln until it glasses over — and the colour you *pull out* is rarely the colour you put in. Each plume went through the kiln two or three times, building up depth, while I kept a record of every firing:

- which powders, in which order
- how long in the heat
- how the colour shifted as it cooled

It took most of three weeks of evenings to get a gradient that ran, plume by plume, from the deepest teal at the centre out to gold at the clasp — the way light actually runs across a feather.

> The colour you pull out of the kiln is rarely the colour you put in. You learn to aim a little to the side of where you want to land.

## Stringing the curve

Then comes the patient part: stringing. The collar is restrung again and again until it falls in a single clean curve and lies flat against the throat without bunching. It is the piece I am least willing to rush, and the one I am most proud to send out.

When you wear it, I hope you feel a little of that green-gold hour — the stillness right before the rain.
