If you have ever stood inside an old haveli in the late afternoon, you will have seen it: light arriving not as a glare but as a scatter of soft shapes across the floor, broken gently by a carved stone screen. That screen is a *jaali*.

## What a jaali is for

A jaali is a piece of practical poetry. It filters harsh sun into something you can sit in. It lets the breeze through on a still day. And it gives a little privacy without shutting the world out entirely — you can see, faintly, without being seen.

I have always thought that was a generous idea: a wall that is also a window.

## Cutting it by hand

To carry that into a ring, the pattern is drawn straight onto the silver. Then each tiny window is drilled, the saw blade is threaded through the hole, and the shape is opened by hand — turning the blade inside spaces barely wider than the metal itself.

A single band holds dozens of these cuts. Every one is made by hand.

> Sawing the windows is the most meditative work I do — right up until the very last one, which always tries to break the blade.

## Negative space as the point

Most jewellery is about what is *there* — the stone, the metal, the shine. A jaali is about what is **not** there: the openings, the gaps, the light that passes through. Wearing one feels, to me, like wearing a small reminder that the empty parts of a thing can be the most beautiful.

Something open, but not entirely.
